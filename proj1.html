System Concept Statement

The Virginia Tech Interactive Museum Experience (VT IMEX) is a system that allows Virginia Tech museum curators to create interactive, meaningful, and informative experiences for museum visitors. The system will offer a method for attaching digital media to physical objects, as well as a method for visitors to access this media alongside the physical object. VT IMEX will solve the issue of the limited informative and interactive properties of traditional museum displays. This system will create an experience for visitors that creates a memorable museum visit that provides a lasting impact on their connection with the world.

Contextual Inquiry

Preparation

The first step we took in our process of gathering work data was identifying where we would collect that data. We decided that with the topic of our project, we would want to gain insight from both visitors to museums and staff that curates them. This would provide data not just for what the average person finds meaningful when they visit museums, but what the users who would use our system find useful or important when creating the experiences for the visitors. Museum exhibits are, after all, a human product with hours of work put into them.

Our group decided to conduct a total of three interviews - two with visitors, and one with a curator. The two visitors interviewed were visitors to two different museums. One was a science museum located in Baltimore, Maryland, and the other was a cultural museum in Seattle, Washington. The curator interviewed is the curator of Virginia Tech’s Corps of Cadets museum, a historical museum. These three were decided upon as they would provide a wide range of perspectives on how museums currently provide engaging experiences to visitors across different subject matters.

Along with the interviews, our group visited various museums to gain first-hand knowledge of how information is presented to visitors. These were the Smithsonian Air & Space Museum and Smithsonian Design Museum. We also visited the Virginia Tech Museum of Geosciences as a group during the contextual inquiry process. As with the interviews, these selections gave us a wide-range in terms of scope. The Air & Space Museum, for example, is vast with a plethora of interactive activities. The Virginia Tech Museum of Geosciences takes up a single lab-sized room in Derring Hall, with only a couple interactive options. Through this, we would be able to gain information on what a museum can offer with a large amount of space and funding, as well as limited resources.

While more interviews and visits and observational inquiry would be desirable, the amount of contextual inquiry we took on was as much as seemed reasonable for our group’s available resources. The main limiter was time and distance. Blacksburg only offers a handful of physical museums to be able to visit, and it is rather difficult to arrange interviews or work practice observation without prior connections in the related fields.

Process
The interviews took place independently. As a group, we came up with separate interview questions for visitors and curators. These were then taken by the team members performing the interviews and conducted as parties were available and willing. Our interview notes were then aggregated to a shared space for team review and discussion.


Questions for museum curators

How do most artifacts come into the museum’s collection? What goes into determining the value of an object to the collection?
What is the decision making process like when considering an exhibit? When creating a display, what goes into consideration in terms of organization and arrangement?
Is there any information or historical context that is often left behind in a physical display? What sorts of common tools or design elements do you use when creating an physical exhibit?
What information do you think museum goers are most interested in when visiting? Do you use any system, tools, etc., that allow visitors to learn more information beyond what’s physically at the museum?
How do you market the museum, or increase its visibility?

Questions for museum goers

What brings you to the museum? How did you hear about this exhibit?
What were your favorite things about the exhibit? What were your least favorite things about the exhibit?
How in-depth do you want to be informed of the pieces in the exhibit? What did you have questions about that you were unable to get an answer to while visiting the museum?
Do you plan on visiting the museum again? Have you been here before
What kind of person do you think would enjoy the exhibit? What would make you want to come back next week?
What exhibits bored you? Why did they bore you? What was something you learned at this museum, if anything?

Raw contextual data was collected via museum visits. The primary one was the Virginia Tech Museum of Geosciences. As a group, we explored this museum for every detail. We took notes and pictures of most displays in the museum, with a focus on interactive objects. We also made note of how a visitor is supposed to interact with the exhibits, and how a visitor actually interacts with them. The display of contextual information related to the exhibits to the visitor was another area of interest in our visits, along with any methods that expand the availability of information beyond the physical display in the museum space (e.g. links to Internet resources). This provided us with valuable information regarding what current work practice in terms of user interaction at a small museum is like.

Contextual Analysis

Final description/understanding of the overall picture
From our findings we learned a lot of information. By interviewing many candidates who visited interactive museums and researching about interactive museums, we noticed that interactive museums enhance the user experience. Activities that target senses (touch, hearing, visual displays comprised of a lot of graphics and many colors) help the user remember the experience. People are also more engaged when they have something to take back with them, such as notes about each exhibit/what they have learned. Notes are good for learning the material. Pictures are great for remembering the memory and recollecting the exhibits. Videos are good for memories and learning information. Souvenirs are great for memories because they’re good reminders of the museum. Group activities are fantastic in that they engage the visitor in conversations about the topic, which enriches their learning experience. The three step process is a great way to affect visitors; notice importance, reach them through senses, and help them take memories back with them. These things are crucial for an interactive experience. Overall, the purpose of a museum is to educate visitors, and the more interactive a museum is, the more likely that the visitors will retain the information.

Requirements Extraction

We extracted the following requirements based on our findings:

  • The system shall offer a tailored experience based on user’s preferences and demographics
  • Curators shall be able to design exhibits.
  • Curators shall be able to interface with a wide-range of hardware. Hardware shall appeal to multiple senses.
  • Curators shall be able to create responsive exhibits
  • Curators shall be able to provide immediate access to additional information beyond the physical space
  • A system to allow user feedback shall be available for visitors at any point during interactions with the system
  • The system shall be able to know its location within the physical space
  • Visitors shall be presented a checklist of what exhibits they have seen
  • Curators shall be able to provide unique visualizations of data to visitors
  • Visitors shall be able to learn more about exhibits before visiting them
  • Curators shall be able to allow visitors to use the system to request more information from staff
  • Curators shall be able to allow employees to manage interactive exhibits in real-time
  • System Requirements
  • Safety
  • The system shall have limitations to allow for visitors’ safety.

Design-Informing Model

Flow Model



The flow model can be separated into four different components:
Exhibits - this includes the creation of the museum exhibits, the setup and logistics of the exhibits, and the organization of different events to entice people to come to the museum. The exhibit creator designs the different exhibits and ensures their high level of interactivity.
Advertising - this encompasses all parts of the work domain involved in informing the audience about what is going on in the museum. Those working in advertising make sure to draw our audience in to the museum.
Operations - this includes all employees involved in keeping the business stable, such as managing its financials, running the day to day operations, dealing with customer issues, etc.
Sales - this includes all portions of the work domain involved in generating revenue for the museum. The website makes it simple for customers to buy tickets, the ticket sellers give tickets to the customers, the ticket buyers supply the revenue to the museum.
All of these four components come together to create a complex system as modeled in our flow diagram.
