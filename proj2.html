The analysis phase of our project focused on primarily Blacksburg’s museums. With the results of our contextual inquiry and analysis, we had a large amount of useful data. It also became apparent that a further refinement of our scope would be beneficial, and lead to an improved design for this phase. We decided to focus our efforts on designing a system for the Virginia Tech Museum of Geosciences. We selected this museum due to its educational opportunities inherent in its material, its physical proximity, and team members’ collective familiarity with it. From here, the design process itself allowed us to tailor our focus further.


Primary Persona



Our primary design persona is Luke Johnson, a 16 year old junior at Blacksburg High School. He is a quarterback on the football team who spends a lot of time with his friends and not much with academics; science is his least favorite subject. Luke is required to visit the Museum of Geosciences for class and has to document his findings. This task is difficult since he is easily bored by scientific concepts and dry presentations and thinks museums are lame.


We chose this persona because we think this is going to be the toughest demographic to target. Younger children are much easier to keep entertained, so if we are able to engage a 16 year old who has no interest in museums or science, our new system can appeal to anyone. 16 year old boys are usually required to go to a museum (school field trips, etc.); to promote learning for them, they need a lot of fun and interaction. Their rebelliousness and disinterest in learning experiences such as museums make them the primary candidate to target because gaining their interest will likely also gain interest of secondary candidates.


User Activities and Tasks


Education - need to teach visitor new information, or new approaches to old information
Hands-on interaction - visitors have direct control over aspects of system
Offers “newness” - visitors are more interested in unique, novel things in museums
Feedback - visitors should be able to provide feedback to the museum curation staff
Social activity - users of the app should be able to compare their score with friends, and share their experiences

Ideation and Sketching


For our ideation process, we thought of different exhibits and activities we encountered at the Museum of Geosciences and how we can apply augmented reality to them. For instance, one of the activities we came up with was an interactive mineral display. Instead of just having information on a sign next to a rock in the museum, the user now points their tablet’s camera view towards the rock and can learn about the rock, view a map of where it was found, zoom in and look at molecular and crystal structure, and link to related minerals. At the museum we found paper handouts for a scavenger hunt, so another idea is a scavenger hunt that incorporates augmented reality. The sketching process goes hand in hand with ideation. Once we come up with an idea that we think has value, we discuss how it would look and feel and quickly generate a sketch to communicate our ideas further among each other.

Designer Mental Model

What the system is
Our system is an interactive app for smartphones that visitors to museums can download and use. It offers a new way of absorbing information at a museum. It presents the physical museum space as a space full of knowledge that must be collected and synthesized by the user.
In the form of a scavenger hunt, a user is presented with a list of objectives to be completed, which are set by museum staff. Users complete this scavenger hunt and their score is kept which they can share with their friends.
There are two main portions to the system - the user side and the museum side. The user side is presented via a smartphone app, which contains information and status relevant to the user (available objectives, completed objectives, score, etc). The museum side is a combination of physical markers in the museum and the museum curated activities which these markers activate when interacted with by the user.

Conceptual Design

Workspace: Our workspace was the computer science lounge. We worked at a large table and had paper and pens for sketching and a whiteboard for organizing ideas.

Ecological conceptual design
The mobile application will be available on both users personal devices and on museum provided devices
The provided tablet will be designed for use by all age grips. Shown below:



The tablet will have a rear-facing camera for augmented reality features
The tablet will have a grip/protective case to accommodate younger children, and a strap to allow kids to wear the device around their neck or shoulder and be hands-free
The museum displays are visibly identified with markers which can be scanned by devices with the app installed
Markers are a mix of scannable QR codes and NFC tags
The user manually initiates scanning via the app
Objectives must be selected by user on the app first before interacting with the marker to prevent simply scanning each one found and hoping it completes an objective
The app interacts with third-party social media to share data the user wishes to share regarding their experience with the app
There is a feature within the app to leave feedback, which is accessible to museum curation staff via the software they use to manage the system

Emotional conceptual design
The app will be able to tailor itself to a user’s preferred difficulty setting
This is selected upon opening the app, but user can switch at any time during their usage in the app
Museum staff can manage how each setting affects the available activities within the app
Keeps users in ideal middle ground between bored and frustrated with the challenge level, maximizing positive response
Museums can control graphics and “look & feel” based on difficulty settings, so easier ones for kids can be more colorful and bright to allow for an engaging and happy experience
When an objective or activity is completed, the user is notified of their increased score and presented with a message of encouragement


Completed objectives remain visible on the app, providing a sense of progression and accomplishment

A user’s current score is visible on the main screen at all times, allowing an easy view of their progress through the activities at a glance
Users will be able to take a culminating quiz after they’ve completed a certain number of objectives. Successfully passing this quiz will permanently mark that user’s profile with a medal of accomplishment, and the museum can offer a prize for this at their discretion.
App allows users to share their score and progress on social media networks
App can also connect to social media and find friend’s/follower’s scores to compare their achievements
Allows comparisons, competition between friends, encouraging continued use of the app in a positive way

Interaction conceptual design
App informs the user upon first activation with basic instructions on how to proceed (how to complete objectives, what to look for, etc)
User is presented with a checklist upon beginning the scavenger hunt with a list of available objectives
The objectives can be completed in any order
Each is designed by museum staff to highlight information the curator finds suitable for a specific age group
Can simply be finding specific marker(s) in the museum, completing an activity through the app, or mix of the two
Checklist allows user to keep track of both completed objectives and opportunities available
App has built-in features to run graphical applications via a common platform (Unity, Flash)
These integrated applications can be set to be available at any time, or only run upon completion of specific objectives
Allows extensibility for museum curators to design their own personal methods of presenting information and allowing interaction between museum and visitor without restrictions of physical space
System offers quizzes (designed by museum staff) after user has completed enough objectives in scavenger hunt
Users are alerted that they can via pop-up once enough points have been accumulated that this is available
App keeps track of a user’s collected artifacts and activities, accessible via the app menu
User can go back and look up information on physical objects and displays they’ve found in their scavenger hunt and otherwise, as well as play activities/games to which these objects are linked

Our conceptual design links our broad mental model of how the system is envisioned to a concrete, physical implementation. It does this by providing explicit parameters for what features are available and how the system will direct users to use these features. It also lays out the notifications the system will use inform users of changing state. This shifts the user’s cognitive process from having to figure out how to use the app to actually learning via their interaction with the museum.
