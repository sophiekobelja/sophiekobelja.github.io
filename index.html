Systems Concept Statement


The Virginia Tech Interactive Museum Experience (VT IMEX) is a system that allows Virginia Tech museum curators to create interactive, meaningful, and informative experiences for museum visitors. The system will offer a method for attaching digital media to physical objects, as well as a method for visitors to access this media alongside the physical object. VT IMEX will solve the issue of the limited informative and interactive properties of traditional museum displays. This system will create an experience for visitors that creates a memorable museum visit that provides a lasting impact on their connection with the world.

Prototyping


For this activity, we created a prototype of a scavenger hunt that would be applied within the Geosciences Museum at Virignia Tech. The prototype would take the user through a task and provide corresponding information based on that task. After discussing with our client, Stephen, he directed us in ensuring the the prototype would be an enriching experience for the users.

Based on our analyses from the previous phase, we knew that the implementation would best be geared toward tablet use (allows portability, simple and efficient interaction). To promote simplicity and avoid constant screen view switches, we designed the prototype for strictly landscape view. The tablet would have some program running that allows the user to participate in the interactive scavenger hunt. We also agreed that aesthetics would be a major factor in the user experience of interactivity. Powerpoint makes it easy to create a highly graphical prototype quite easily. Therefore, we used Powerpoint to implement the scavenger hunt simulation. Our hopes would be that this type of prototype would provide us with the best results for evaluations. This way we can hand the user a tablet with the slides, and they can be truly immersed in the experience as if they were actually using the final product.

Prototyping Goals

Prior Goals Connection to Prototype
Improve retention of information from visiting museum App ends with quiz after scavenger hunt which offers prizes based on information remembered
Incentivize return trips to museum Scavenger hunt differs dependent upon age range and current exhibits offered. Better prizes for better score in the scavenger hunt.
Bring nontraditional museum patrons to museum Leaderboard inspires competition and people will want to come back to get the highscore
Improve information for each exhibit Scanning QR code brings up virtual hub of information and videos and stores that information in an “inventory” to look up later
Improve group communication within museum Create point system to invoke competition among teams during scavenger hunt

Prototyping Process To build our prototype, we used PowerPoint to link static slides together through hyperlinks. We wanted to simulate the major tasks that would be carried out with the tablet. We settled on implementing the execution of one task from the scavenger hunt, and showing information about one item in the inventory, as the remaining functionality of the tablet is essentially a derivative of these tasks.
The prototype allows the user to start the scavenger hunt and view the task screen. Then, the user can start the task of finding a volcanic rock. When it finds the rock a small animation is shown simulating the scanning of a QR code next to said rock. Then, the user gains a new item in their inventory and is able to navigate to the inventory and view more information about the rock, including an animation showing how volcanic rocks are formed.

Evaluation

Evaluation Plan Introduction
For our evaluation, our team developed a plan that would allow us to gauge primary metrics which were our goals in the design of the prototype. These goals arose back in the analysis phase of the lifecycle. From the user activities we discovered through inquiry, these have followed us through the entire UX design process and have informed our design.

We decided to rework our metrics and goals a bit from Phase 2 to produce this more complete set of information what we wanted to gain from our design. These would better test our design and ultimate project goals.

Goals
User education on topic - We wanted to gauge whether the user would feel that the system helped them learn about the material covered by the museum.
Positive emotional response to activity - Our team wanted to answer the question: Is it fun or is it a chore? The system should be fun and engaging, not simply another assignment or mindless distraction.
Ease of use - The system should be intuitive with a low learning curve so that as many visitors as possible can pick up a tablet and complete it without frustrations.
Willingness to return to museum - We wanted the app to provide an incentive to come back to the museum for multiple visits. It should not be a “one and done” gimmick for the museum, but an integral part of how it informs visitors.

Tasks to Complete
Our design was a broad one in the previous phase. For this phase, after narrowing our goals down to these four, we picked a specific set of tasks whose data would offer information of value to our analysis.
There were three main tasks we wanted the users to complete:
  • Complete an activity
  • Examine an artifact in the inventory
  • Take the final quiz

These tasks would be our benchmark tasks, and would offer the best insight into how well our prototype met our goals. We also added a few other tasks for users which would be more navigational tasks. These were not primary goals, but allowed the prototype to be properly evaluated while highlight issues with our layout and visual design.

Evaluation Procedures
Our evaluation was designed as a beginning-to-end process for each participant. With feedback we gained from the in-class evaluation activity, we developed a streamlined and complete plan that a single team representative would be able to use to guide a user through the evaluation. We decided on this plan as a compromise between prototype capabilities, logistical concerns, and needs of the project. It would allow us to quickly and effectively perform the evaluations while considering the realities in scheduling, the project timeline, and site availability.
The logistics of each evaluation was an individual meeting set up between a participant and a design team contact. All the evaluation required was a quiet space and access to our prototype on a laptop computer. The team member would read the introduction and scenario to the participant, and then begin the actual evaluation.
The team member would provide the participant with a task, and observe them as they performed it. Any notes on how they completed it, any issues, or any notes voiced by the participant were written down by the evaluator. At certain points, the team member would offer extra context to their tasks to simulate actually being in the museum.
The final task was to complete a quiz. This was not implemented in our prototype, but was instead an oral examination of two questions. The purpose behind this was to gauge how useful the inventory was in the quiz. The participant had access to information via the prototype and the team member would record how or if they used this when answering the questions.
When the evaluation was complete, there was an oral questionnaire performed by the team member. It consisted of nine statements which the participant would rate on a scale of 1 to 5 how much they agreed with it. They were then asked for any frustrations they experienced with the prototype, and finally any comments or observations at all.

Evaluation Results

Results Introduction
What follows are the results from our evaluations. A total of eight Virginia Tech undergraduate students participated in this evaluation. Each session was completed individually and took around ten minutes to complete.

General Observations
The “i” button on the items in the inventory seemed like a good marker for information
Everyone clicked through all information slides without prompting
Replayability concerns popped up often
Multiple complaints about visibility of some actions, such as X/close button in inventory
Should we have had participants vocalize their simulated physical actions (like “I go here” or “I hold up my tablet to my friend”)? More think aloud? Changed third task to more general “Share your find with your friend”)? Added a 3.5 task (go to main screen)?
Participants didn’t seem to know you could use inventory for help during the quiz
